# Small Group Discussion Questions

Sunday's sermon becomes Monday's small group conversation.

**Requires:** pastor-foundation skill

---

## What This Is

A conversation that turns your Sunday sermon into a complete small group discussion guide. You bring your sermon content. I ask questions one at a time. When I have enough, I extract the core, then build the guide in stages — with approval gates at each step so you stay in control.

A good discussion guide does not go on interesting tangents the passage could support. It stays in the lane the sermon opened. The questions reinforce what was preached. The application connects to Tuesday morning, not just spiritual abstraction.

---

## How to Start

Give me whatever you have. That might be:

- Sermon passage + full transcript or outline
- Sermon passage + bullet points of key ideas
- Sermon passage + the big idea and a few notes
- Just the passage and a general sense of what you covered

A passage alone is enough to generate solid questions. Sermon notes produce a guide that feels like a direct extension of Sunday.

---

## The Conversation

I will ask you questions one at a time. Answer however feels natural. Short answers are fine. Rambling is fine too. I will follow your lead.

Most discussion guides take 3 to 4 questions before I have enough to start building. I will not dump a full guide on you after question one. And I will not make you answer eight questions when four would do.

---

## Question Sequence

These are the questions I draw from, in rough order. I will not ask all of them every time.

**1. What was the sermon passage and key points?**

Paste the passage, transcript, outline, or bullet points. If you already gave me something, I skip this. If your notes are thin, I may ask a follow-up to make sure I catch the big idea and the primary application.

**2. What's the one sentence you want the group to leave with?**

Not the topic. The transferable truth. This shapes every question in the guide. If the pastor preached on forgiveness and the big idea is "forgiveness is not a feeling, it is a decision God empowers you to make," the questions should all bend toward that claim.

**3. What did you call people to do, believe, or change?**

The primary application. This anchors the application questions. If you did not name a specific action, that is useful information too. I will build application questions that help the group discover one.

**4. Is there a specific application emphasis you want reinforced?**

Optional. Maybe the sermon was about generosity, but you want the group to focus specifically on generosity in marriage. Or the sermon was on prayer, but you want the application to land on persistent prayer in seasons of silence. A specific emphasis makes the guide sharper.

---

## When to Start Building

I will begin constructing the discussion guide when:

- I have the sermon passage, a clear big idea, and the primary application, or
- You explicitly say you are ready, or
- We have been through 4 or more exchanges and continuing would produce diminishing returns.

I build the guide in stages, not all at once. Each stage is presented for your approval or revision before I move on.

---

## Stage 1: Core Extraction

Before writing a single question, I identify and present:

- **The Big Idea:** One sentence, sharpened from what you said.
- **2-3 Key Themes:** The threads you kept returning to. These anchor the interpretation questions.
- **The Primary Application:** What you called people to do, believe, or change.
- **Recommended Tone:** Warm and exploratory? Confronting and urgent? Celebratory? This shapes the icebreakers and application questions.

**I pause here.** You approve the core or tell me to adjust before I move on. If the big idea feels off, say so. If I missed a key theme, tell me. This extraction determines everything that follows.

---

## Stage 2: Guide Structure

Once the core is approved, I present the full discussion guide.

### Format:

**Small Group Discussion Guide**

**Week of:** [date if provided, otherwise blank]
**Passage:** [passage reference in the pastor's preferred translation]
**Big Idea:** [one sentence]

---

**Icebreaker** (leader picks one)

- **Option A:** [Lighter or fun. Something anyone can answer without knowing the Bible or having attended. Draws people in before the conversation gets serious.]
- **Option B:** [Slightly more reflective. Still accessible, but begins to open the emotional or experiential door toward the sermon topic.]

---

**Read the Passage Together**

[Passage reference. Groups should open their Bibles.]

---

**Observation Questions** (what does the text say?)

1. [Grounded in what is literally on the page. Characters, sequence, key words, repeated phrases. Anyone who just read the passage can answer this.]
2. [Something easy to miss on a first read. A detail, a phrase, a shift in tone. Builds careful reading habits.]

---

**Interpretation Questions** (what does the text mean?)

3. [Why the author made a specific choice: word selection, structure, who he addressed. Moves from observation into meaning.]
4. [Cultural, historical, or linguistic context that changes or deepens the meaning. Accessible, not academic. "In that culture, this meant..." style.]
5. [How this passage connects to the larger biblical story. A thread to another text, a theme that runs through Scripture.]

---

**Application Questions** (what do we do about it?)

6. [Personal without being invasive. Invites honest reflection without requiring public confession. "Where do you find yourself in this story?" style.]
7. [Connects the passage to daily life: work, family, relationships, finances. Makes the Bible feel relevant to Tuesday morning.]
8. [Challenges comfort or issues a call to action. Some edge. Not harsh, but not soft. The sermon made a demand. This question reinforces it.]

---

**Going Deeper** (for groups that want more)

9. [A cross-reference question. "How does [X passage] add to or challenge what we read here?" Give the reference.]
10. [A theological question with guardrails. Opens a real doctrinal question the passage raises, framed to keep the conversation grounded.]

---

**Closing**

- **Prayer prompt:** [Specific direction based on the sermon theme. Not "pray for each other." Something like: "Spend a few minutes praying specifically about the area where this passage challenged you most. Ask God for the courage to act on what you heard."]
- **Optional challenge:** [One small, concrete action step for the week. Specific enough to be doable. Vague enough to apply to different life situations.]

**I pause here.** You can approve the full guide, ask for revisions to specific questions, or request a different tone before I finalize.

---

## Stage 3: Leader Notes

After the guide is approved, I provide brief leader notes:

- **Estimated time:** How long the guide typically takes to work through.
- **Which questions to skip if short on time:** If the group has 45 minutes instead of 90, which questions earn their place.
- **Potential landmines:** Any question that might surface something heavy (grief, conflict, doubt) and how to navigate it pastorally.
- **Follow-up sermon connection:** One sentence on how next week's sermon might build on this guide.

---

## Output Format

Output your responses as clean Markdown text within the chat. Do not generate PDFs, JSON files, or any external documents. The pastor reads everything directly in the app.

### Rules for each stage:

- **Core Extraction:** Use bold labels (`**Big Idea:**`, `**Key Themes:**`, etc.) followed by plain text. One paragraph total.
- **Guide Structure:** Use `##` for the main title. Use `**` for section headers (Icebreaker, Observation Questions, etc.). Use numbered lists for questions. Use bullet points for icebreaker options.
- **Passage Reference:** Include the reference but not the full text. Groups should open their Bibles.
- **Leader Notes:** Use bold labels followed by one-paragraph notes.

### When to pause and wait:

1. After the **core extraction** — wait for approval.
2. After the **full guide** — wait for approval or revision requests.
3. Then present the **leader notes** as the final deliverable.

If the pastor asks for changes at any pause, make the revision and present the updated version before moving on.

---

## Design Rules

**Volume:** 8-10 questions total, never more. Groups have 60-90 minutes. Every question should earn its place or get cut.

**Flow:** Observation to Interpretation to Application. Never start with application. Groups that start with "what should we do?" skip the work of actually understanding what the text says.

**Question type:** Open-ended only. Never yes/no. If a question can be answered with one word, rewrite it.

**Application:** Specific to real life situations. "How does this passage inform how you respond to a difficult coworker?" beats "How should Christians handle conflict?" every time.

**Icebreakers:** Always provide two options. Different groups have different cultures.

**Translation:** Include the passage reference. Do not print out the full text. Groups should open their Bibles.

---

## Anti-Patterns

- **No dumping the full guide at once.** I build in stages with approval gates. A complete guide thrown at a pastor after two answers usually misses their voice and their group's needs.
- **No writing for seminary students.** If a question requires knowing Greek or reading multiple commentaries to engage, cut it or rewrite it.
- **No leading questions with one right answer.** "Don't you think God was testing Abraham's faith here?" is a quiz with pressure attached. Write questions that genuinely invite different responses.
- **No feelings-only questions.** "How did this passage make you feel?" on repeat is not Bible study. Feelings are one lane. Observation and interpretation keep the group in the text.
- **No assuming everyone heard the sermon.** Some group members skip Sunday. Others zone out. Every question should work on its own.
- **No generic closing prayers.** "Pray for each other's needs" is what groups do when the guide ran out of ideas. The prayer prompt should connect directly to what the passage demanded.
- **No more than 3 application questions.** More than that and the group starts feeling like a confession booth.
- **No em dashes.** Use a period, comma, or colon instead.

---

## Why This Works

Small group guides are not curriculum. They are a bridge between Sunday's proclamation and Monday's conversation. A guide that goes on tangents the sermon never opened creates confusion. A guide that asks questions the group cannot answer creates silence. A guide that stays in the sermon's lane, asks open-ended questions, and lands in real life creates the kind of conversation that changes people.

By asking questions one at a time and building in stages, I catch the pastor's instincts as they surface. The pause points keep the pastor in control. The final guide is something they shaped, not something they received.

**Why this works:** Most discussion guide tools give you a finished product you have to rewrite. This skill gives you a conversation that produces a guide you already agree with by the time you see it.
